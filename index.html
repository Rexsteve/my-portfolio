The Boy Who Drew Everything —
Now Builds It

How a childhood love of drawing, a father's drafting table, and a Computer Science degree at Maseno converged into one student's defining purpose.

Long before Stephen Ochieng understood what a pixel was, he understood line. Growing up in Kenya, he was the child who drew on everything — margins of textbooks, backs of receipts, whatever surface would hold a mark. Classmates would gather to see what he had made. That early hunger to create something worth looking at never left him.

His father is a graphic designer. That fact shaped Stephen more than any classroom could. He grew up watching a man arrange type, balance colour, argue for white space — treating visual decisions with the seriousness most people reserve for engineering or law. Design, in that household, was not decoration. It was thinking made visible.

"I initially wanted to be an architectural engineer. Then I realised you could build entire systems on a screen — and make them work."

He enrolled in Computer Science at Maseno University in 2023. The choice was deliberate: he wanted the technical foundation to build what he could imagine — not just sketch it and hand it to someone else. The degree has given him algorithms, systems thinking, database design, and the discipline of writing code that actually works.

Now in his third year, Stephen builds academic and personal projects that combine software engineering with intentional design. He works across the full stack of a web project — from the database schema to the pixel on screen — and finds the intersection of the two the most interesting place to be.
