The Boy Who Drew Everything —
Now Builds It

How a childhood love of drawing, a father's drafting table, and a Computer Science degree converged into one young designer's defining purpose.

Long before Stephen Otieno understood what a pixel was, he understood line. Growing up in Kenya, he was the child who drew on everything — margins of textbooks, backs of receipts, whatever surface would hold a mark. Classmates would crowd to see what he'd made. That early hunger to create something that stopped people, that made them look twice, never left him.

His father is a graphic designer. That fact shaped Stephen more than any classroom. He grew up watching a man arrange type, balance colour, argue for white space — treating visual decisions with the seriousness most people reserve for engineering or law. Design, in that household, was not decoration. It was thinking made visible.

"I always wanted to be an architectural engineer. Then I discovered you could build entire worlds on a screen — and they could move."

For a time, Stephen dreamed of becoming an architectural engineer. He was drawn to scale, structure, and the idea that you could design spaces people would inhabit and feel. That dream evolved rather than disappeared: when he encountered modern digital design — interfaces that breathed, websites that told stories, apps that anticipated what you needed before you asked — he recognized the same discipline, applied to a different kind of space.

He enrolled in Computer Science at Maseno University. The choice was deliberate: he wanted the technical foundation to build what he could imagine, not just design it and hand it to someone else. Now in his third year, Stephen sits at the intersection he has always gravitated toward — the place where logic and creativity are not opposites but collaborators. He designs. He codes. And increasingly, he does both at once.
